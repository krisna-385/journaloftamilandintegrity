Editor's Note

This inaugural issue places two unlikely neighbours on the same shelf. One traces how southern India taught stone, leaf, and clay to hold language — Kuriyeedu graffiti hardening into Tamizhi, Tamizhi branching into Grantha, Vatteluttu, and the modern Tamil page. The other examines what happens when a different kind of record — a procurement file, a laboratory report, an audit trail — is left unguarded, and how an institution as old as Tirumala can be quietly recomposed from within.

We publish them together on purpose. Cangzhi — to carve, to record — is the thread: a script incised in rock survives because no one can secretly edit it; a ghee certificate survives only if someone insists it can't be either. The integrity in our title is not a slogan. It is the shared property both papers are actually studying.

— Editorial Board, Cangzhi

The Ceramic Canvas: Tracing the Epigraphic and Sociolinguistic Evolution of the Tamil Script

Keerthigan Krsnan Gopikrishnan · Curtin University, Malaysia

Follows southern Indian writing from non-phonetic Kuriyeedu graffiti through the allophonic engineering of Tamizhi to the regional divergence of Grantha, Malayalam, and the Kannada–Telugu continuum, arguing for a networked rather than linear model of script evolution.

Scope

Cangzhi publishes work at the intersection of institutional governance and the historical record — procurement and administrative accountability, epigraphy and script history, and the long durability (or failure) of public and material systems of trust.

Review Process

All submissions undergo double-blind peer review by at least two reviewers with relevant disciplinary expertise. Average time to first decision: 6–8 weeks.
